Tribute to a great director and a lover of the heritage of Salona, Bogdan Žižić, with the screening of his films devoted to Salona:

Bogdan Žižić,
Croatian film and television director, screenwriter (Solin, November 8, 1934 – Zagreb, 29 Mrch, 2021)

Žižić graduated at the Law Faculty of Zagreb University. He worked in Zagreb Film as dramaturge from 1960 to 1964 and director from 1984 to 1990. From 1964 he was engaged in film direction, making his name as one of the leading Croatian short films directors, for which he won international prizes. His acted films, with elements of surrealism were Madeleine, mon amour!, 1971; Joke, 1973; Last Race, 1975; In his documentary films, two important themes can be perceived – recent Croatian history and personal testimonies – Jasenovac, 1966; Goli otok, 1994; Damnatio memoriae or an assault on memory, 2000; A Vukovar Story, 2002; Vlado Gotovac – My case, 2008; Jasenovac Memento, 2016, – and the other is fine art and the life of artists – Expressionism in Croatian Painting, 1985; Emanuel Vidović, 1989; Celestin Medović, 1991; Alfred Pal, 1993; Vlado Bukovac, 1994; Dear Vincent, 2006; Leo Junek – painter with two homelands, 2009; Miroslav Kraljević, 2014. His best achievements were in films with no dialogue or accompanying narration, both acted (Journey, 1972; Press, 1981, jury prize at the Oberhausen Festival) and documentary (Praise to the hand, 1968; Morning of a pure body, 1969; The Gastarbeiter (Dragutin Trumbetaš), 1977; Fairground, 1982; One Life, 1985). His feature films are House (1975) and Don’t lean out (1977), both awarded the Grand Gold Arena at the Pula Feature Film Festival, making his name as a leading representative of the trend for socially critical treatments in the Croatian movies in the 1970s. Two other films belong to the same thematic group: Give what you give (1979) and Early Snow in Munich, 1984, his most successful feature film occupied with the generation gap in a family of émigrés in Germany. A not so well received feature film, Price of a Life (1994) is thematically connected with the Homeland War. For television he produced the acted films Quartet (1997, after Milan Begović) and House of Ghosts (1998, after Ksaver Šandor Gjalski), as well as many documentaries about cultural history, especially that of Split and Dalmatia (Along the Ruins of Salona I-II, 2004; Palace of the Sun, 2005; Josip Slade, builder, 2007; A Second Venice, 2010; A century of cinematography in Split, 2011; Along the Cetina River to Trilj, 2011; Ejnar Dyggve – Citizen of Solin, 2016). He published a book of memoirs, Hourglass (2009) as well as a book about Alfred Pall called Burning Bush, 2011 and another about Josip Karaman (Josip Bepo Karaman and the Split of his time, 2012), He won the Vladimir Nazor Lifetime Achievement Prize in 2008. Other important documentary films are Flood (1964), Unbutton (1968) and Ivana (1981).
